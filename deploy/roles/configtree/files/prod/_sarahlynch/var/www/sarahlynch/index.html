Merit Professional Services

Website redesign project for DFW-based Environmental Consulting Firm, Merit Professional Services.

  • Date: January - February 2019
  • Client: Merit Professional Services
  • Category: Desktop Website Redesign

The Company

Merit Professional Services, colloquially referred to as “Merit”, is an Environmental Consulting Firm which offers various services pertaining to the prevention of water pollution at many different types of construction and manufacturing sites.

Users arrive at the Merit Professional home page looking for information about how Merit can help their company with Storm Water Compliance, so that they won’t face severe penalties, including expensive fines.




The Design Challenge

Important Factors to Consider:
1. Merit Professional’s current website uses a Wordpress theme.
2. Merit outsources their web development work.
3. Merit has limited resources when it comes to website work.
4. The company’s logo cannot change.
5. The current CMS theme is functional and has a fairly logical layout.

BUSINESS GOAL: Adding content about Merit’s Training and Certification programs to the website.
Merit wants to monetize these educational services and use them to encourage engagement in their Partnerships.

IBM Conversational Design

A 311-operator-style Chatbot, as part of a larger “Immersive Experience” designed to demonstrate the capabilities of IBM Watson Conversation Service (since renamed to “Watson Assistant”).

  • Date: May - November 2017
  • Client: IBM
  • Category: WCS Chatbot Application





Immersive Experience Overview

Immersive Experiences are demo applications presented to potential clients in Client Experience Centers.
The applications are shown on a large wall of screens, along with additional displays, such as touch tables.
The presentations of Immersive Experiences are led by IBM employees called Engagement Leaders.






Our 311-Reporting Immersive Experience

Design Problem

Design & Implement an immersive experience to educate clients on Watson Conversation Service.

Design Hypothesis

Clients will learn more easily if they can interact with an example WCS Chatbot.

Design Solution

Create an immersive experience simulating a 311 Issue Reporting System, using a 311 Operator Chatbot.

Our Immersive 311 Reporting Application was made up of two parts:
1. Visualizations displayed on the wall of screens, triggered by user interactions with …
2. the 311 Operator Chatbot, on the touch table.


Defining our User Bases


Our Engagement Leader, Gwen, was the primary user of our app’s Chatbot.










My Linguistic Design Challenge


My design challenge was to make a chatbot that:
1. interpreted and routed 311 reports while providing a good user experience, and
2. highlighted Watson Conversation Service capabilities.

This was essentially a Linguistic Design Challenge. Read further to learn about my 311 Chatbot design process!

Designing the Dialog


Crafting a Realistic Conversation Flow


The first question to answer in designing the dialog for this 311 Operator chatbot:
How would a Human Operator handle a 311 report?

A human 311 operator would need to know:
1. What issue is the caller reporting?
2. Where is the issue happening?



The Operator would use these two pieces of information to pass the report on to the appropriate agency to handle it.
I needed to design a dialog flow to mirror the behavior of a human operator.








Segmenting the Dialog


The 311 report dialog flow based off of real Human Operator behavior helped me to determine how the conversation could be segmented: by issue collection, and address collection.

However, I knew that Gwen is used to conversing flexibly.
Gwen might:
1. give an issue report first.
2. give an address first.
3. give both at the same time.



The chatbot needed to be flexible enough to recognize each segment of the conversation, no matter the order.






Scoping & Organizing the Training Data


To learn how to process 311 reports, our 311 bot needed categories to use to classify user input.
I roughly based 311 Bot’s categories off of how New York City’s 311 Reporting System categorizes citizens’ reports.

See NYC’s categories here.





Defining a Hierarchicy of Training Data


My research into NYC’s 311 Reporting System showed that Agencies were assigned according to details in a report.
I constructed training data to train 311 Bot to identify these details by keyword.

I designed 311 Bot’s back-end logic to use these keywords to mimic NYC’s 311 Reporting System’s Agency assignment.






Creating a Memorable Experience


I also used this keyword framework to create a good chat user experience.
By carefully crafting variations of these keywords (training data), I created a 311 Bot that was able to occasionally surprise users when it recognized words within their utterances.

Amazon Kindle Community

A General Assembly spec project, to design a forum-based platform for literary discussion.

  • Date: September 2018
  • Client: Amazon Kindle Community (Goodreads)
  • Category: Mobile App Redesign

Design Problem

The Community feature is a wrapper for a pared-down version of Goodreads, a company bought by Amazon in 2013.
It enables users to view and comment on book reviews.

Kindle iOS App users are dissatisfied with the current “Community” feature.

Design Hypothesis

Kindle App users would rather have the ability to read & participate in discussions on books with other readers.

Design Solution

Enable users to find, read, and participate in discussions about books, by designing a forum-based discussion platform to replace the current Community feature.







Case Study Sections

Jump Links:















User Research:

I helped implement multiple types of User Research for this project, including:
1. conducting an Online Survey,
2. conducting in-person User Interviews, and
3. analyzing User Reviews of the GoodReads app.

Through this research, we learned that users were dissatisfied with the review/rating functionality of GoodReads.



We also gained insight into how users behave on discussion platforms (social media, forums, etc.).
We found that behavioral patterns were split:
Some of our users were active participators. Others preferred to observe.



Interview with Subject Matter Expert:

I recruited a user who served as my Subject Matter Expert for this project.
She is an avid reader and has attended multiple bookclubs over the years. We spoke in depth about her experience discussing what she read with other readers.

My SME described her love for hearing how stories affected other readers, and how their interpretations differed in unique and interesting ways. She lamented about how book club attendance had dwindled and how new clubs were few and far between.

RailYard

Web presence redesign project for Austin-based telecom provider aggregator company, RailYard.

  • Date: September 2018
  • Client: RailYard
  • Category: Desktop Website & Dashboard Redesign

Design Problem

Redesign RailYard's web presence to better target the company's Marketing Personas.

Design Challenge

Find the UX Design work in the Marketing Project Prompt.
Improve look & feel and content to target Marketing Personas, while also improving the User Experience for the Main User Base of RailYard's Dashboard.

Design Solution

Balance Marketing goals with User Experience goals by:
1. Redesigning the look & feel and content of Home Page, and
2. Assessing & Improving UX of Dashboard.

INTRO: USER PERSONAS


MARKETING PERSONAS

In this project, my team was directly tasked with updating the look & feel and the content of the RailYard Home Page to better target two Marketing Personas.

Based on these curated Marketing Personas described by our stakeholders, my team was able to formulate two User Personas.





SMALL BUSINESS OWNER

As UX Designers, we couldn’t leave out evaluating the user experience of RailYard’s main product, their Dashboard.
The Dashboard’s primary user base is the Small Business Owner.

User Persona

My team used this interview to construct a User Persona representing the Small Business Owner.
A lot of my work in this project focused on the user experience of this User Persona.











As-Is Assessment

The RailYard Home Page held the primary avenue for reaching the RY Dashboard: an address search bar.



Since this Home Page-to-Dashboard workflow was so key to the Business Owner’s experience, I wanted to evaluate its current design.

There were two main points in this workflow experience that I thought could be greatly improved upon:
1. address display inconsistency, and
2. non-functioning address suggestions.


Inconsistent Addresses from Home Page to Dashboard

When the User is taken to the dashboard, the address displayed does not exactly match the address the user just selected from the dropdown on the Home Page.





Misleading Suggestions

Because not all possible addresses included in the dropdown were functional, it was possible for a user to start typing an address in the search bar, select an auto-completed address suggested to them in the dropdown list, and then attempt to hit ‘Go’…




… and end up very confused because nothing would happen.


Addressing Small Business Owner Needs

Tying this assessment back to my findings from the interview with our Small Business Owner:



The current design of RailYard’s Dashboard addressed our Business Owner’s first need: displaying ISP info in one place.
I concluded after my as-is assessment that the second need could be addressed better.

CARD SORT

I wanted to investigate user instincts surrounding the filter labels used in the Dashboard.

To conduct these Card Sorts, I presented each participant of our user tests with a list of items that belonged to a category, and asked them what they thought the name of the category should be.




I asked this question twice, once for each of the two different categories I wanted to investigate.




The summary of the participants’ feedback on the names of these categories were:
1. ‘Primary Usage’ for the first category, and
2. ‘Reason for Shopping’ for the second category.

Trading Filters for a Form:

Our stakeholders confirmed that the user’s specified requirements for the Dashboard filters - Timeframe, Primary Usage, Reason for Shopping, & Company Size - should cause the displayed provider list to refresh …
but their back-end was not yet technically capable of accomplishing a refresh.

We decided that collecting these pieces of information via filters on the dashboard interface without the functionality to support them, would not be a good user experience.

So, we decided to move this information collection to a short form in the “Know Before You Go” modal.
This way, the information could still be collected and passed along as the user traveled to the Provider’s site.

Next Steps

If my team had had more time to begin a second iteration for this project, we would have performed tests to
1) validate home page copy, and 2) validate placement of filters on the dashbaord.


Home Page Copy Validation

The first major component of a second iteration on this project would include a series of user tests to validate the copy we were given by our stakeholders to include in the Home Page.
After isolating a group of representative users, we would ask each participant the following questions while they were actively looking at the Home Page.

Copy Validation Interview Questions:
1. Visit RailYard.com’s HomePage
2. Scroll through the homepage. What do you think this company does?
3. Where would you find more information on how RailYard’s offerings can benefit your business?
4. Does the information in the “CRE/Provider” section encourage you to consider partnering with RailYard?
5. Where would you expect to find contact information for RailYard?
6. How would you get in touch with RailYard?
7. Do you believe that partnering with RailYard would benefit you?
8. Is there any information you’d like to see here, that isn’t here already?

We would then take users’ feedback during these interviews to iterate on the copy and content included in the Home Page.



A/B Testing of “Filter” Placement

The other major component of a second iteration on this project would be a series of A/B tests to investigate the effectiveness of the placement of filters on the top of the Dashboard vs. included in the ‘Know Before You Go’ modal.

We would measure the number of conversions of users traveling to provider sites with each option for placement of the filters, and report back to our stakeholders which placement option proved most effective.
